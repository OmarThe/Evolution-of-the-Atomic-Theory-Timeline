Aristotle

Around 350 B.C.E., Aristotle (384-322 B.C.E.), an influential Greek thinker at the time,
disagreed with Democritus' atomic concepts. Instead, they believed that matter was made up of
varying amounts of fire, air, earth, and water and that there were no smallest parts to matter.
Because there was no method to assess the relative merits of opposing concepts in an
experimental setting, Aristotle's viewpoint won over, owing to his influence. For approximately
2000 years, the idea that matter is made up of atoms took a back place in intellectual thought.
