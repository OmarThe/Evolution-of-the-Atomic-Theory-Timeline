Murray Gell-Mann and George Zweig

Murray Gell-Mann

In 1952, Murray Gell-Mann discovered a new class of elementary particles known as cosmic
ray particles (now called kaons and hyperons). After discovering these particles, Einstein
proposed quantum chromodynamics, a theory that describes the strong force's behaviour.
Quantum chromodynamics was developed in the same way that quantum electrodynamics, or the
quantum field theory of electromagnetic force, was created. According to this idea, subatomic
particles like electrons, protons, and neutrons are built up of "quarks."

George Zweig

George Zweig was the first to notice mathematical constructions that explained regularities
in early particle-scattering tests' results. This was crucial in his quest for quarks. In
1964, George Zweig was the first to introduce quarks, which laid the groundwork for particle
physics. Like Murray Gell- Mann's, particles' realized features could be explained by treating
them as triplets of other constituent particles.
