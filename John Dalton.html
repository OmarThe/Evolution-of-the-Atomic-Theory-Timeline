John Dalton

By the early 1800s, certain observations led the English chemist John Dalton (1766-1844) to
offer convincing evidence that supported the early atomic ideas of Leucippus and Democritus.
The three most important laws that led to the development and acceptance of the atomic
theory are as follows:

  • The law of conservation of mass.
  • The law of definite proportions.
  • The law of multiple proportions.

In 1804, John Dalton published his law of multiple proportions, which claims the following principle:

  • When two elements (call them A and B) form two different compounds, the masses of element B
    that combine with 1 g of element A can be expressed as a ratio of small whole numbers.

In 1808, John Dalton explained the laws just discussed with his atomic theory, which included
the following concepts:

  1. Each element is composed of tiny indestructible particles called Atoms.
  2. All atoms of a given element have the same mass and physical/chemical properties that
    distinguish them from the atoms of other elements.
  3. Atoms combine in simple, whole-number ratios to form compounds.
  4. Atoms of one element cannot change into atoms of another element. In a chemical reaction,
    atoms only change how they are bound together with other atoms.

Dalton imagined atoms as solid, hard spheres, similar to billiard (pool) balls because he believed
they were the smallest particles of matter. He used wooden balls to simulate them. He also suggested
that atoms can rearrange themselves to form new substances in chemical reactions; however, atoms can
never be created or destroyed. Dalton also suggested the use of symbols for atoms of different elements.
