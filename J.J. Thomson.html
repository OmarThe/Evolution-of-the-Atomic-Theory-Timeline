J.J. Thomson

The Discovery of the Electron

J. J. Thomson (1856-1940), an English physicist working at Cambridge University in 1897, conducted experiments to
investigate the properties of cathode rays. Thomson created a cathode ray tube, which is a glass tube. The tube had been
partially evacuated, which meant that some of the air had been forced out (because air interferes with cathode rays).
Thomson then used two electrodes at either end of the tube to provide a high electrical voltage. Thomson discovered that
cathode rays pass from a negatively charged electrode (the cathode) to a positively charged electrode (the anode).

Thomson found that these particles had the following properties:

  • They travelled in straight lines.
  • They were independent of the composition of the material from which they originated (the cathode).
  • They carried a negative electrical charge.

J. J. Thomson used electric and magnetic fields to deflect cathode ray particles and quantify their charge-to-mass ratio.
The cathode ray particle was around 2000 times lighter (less massive) than hydrogen, the lightest known atom, based on
the value he measured, 1.76 X 10^8 coulombs (C) per gram. These findings were astoundingâ€”it appeared that the indestructible
atom could apparently be chipped! J. J. Thomson had discovered the electron.

The Structure of the Atom

J. J. Thomson proposed that the negatively charged electrons were small particles held within a positively charged
sphere. The most popular model of the time became known as the plum-pudding model.
